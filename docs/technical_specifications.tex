% ======== 1. REQUIREMENT UNDERSTANDING ========
\section{Requirement Understanding}

\subsection{Problem Framing}

Urban traffic congestion in Dubai costs an estimated AED~4.6 billion annually in lost productivity, fuel waste, and environmental damage [6]. The Dubai Roads and Transport Authority (RTA) manages over 700 signalized intersections, the majority operating on fixed-timer schedules that cannot adapt to dynamic traffic conditions --- rush-hour surges, lane-closure incidents, special events, or emergency vehicle preemption. Traditional fixed-cycle signal control is demonstrably suboptimal under non-stationary traffic patterns [1]. This system addresses the problem through a simulated \textbf{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL)} environment in which 16 intersection controllers independently learn optimal signal timing policies via Q-Learning and SARSA over a 200-step time horizon, evaluated by WSM and TOPSIS multi-criteria decision analysis [4][5]. The design integrates research on IoT-based smart traffic systems, CNNs for vehicle detection, and emergency vehicle prioritization [1][2].

\subsection{Agent Roles and Architecture}

Each of the 16 intersections operates as a \textbf{decentralized RL agent} with a private Q-table. Agents do not communicate directly but interact through shared traffic dynamics --- vehicles departing one intersection arrive at adjacent ones. Three control strategies are evaluated:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{llll}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Agent} & \textbf{Algorithm} & \textbf{Policy Type} & \textbf{Behavioural Trait} \\
    \midrule
    Q-Learning & Tabular TD (off-policy) & Aggressive; $\max Q(s',a')$ & Fast convergence; may overestimate Q-values \\
    SARSA & Tabular TD (on-policy) & Conservative; actual $Q(s',a')$ & Safer policies; accounts for exploration noise \\
    Fixed Timer & Deterministic cycle & No learning (baseline) & Switches phase every 15 steps; no state awareness \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{System Objectives}

The system targets five measurable performance criteria, aligned with Dubai RTA operational benchmarks [6]:

\begin{itemize}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Wait Time Reduction} $\geq 10\%$ vs.\ fixed-timer baseline in dynamic scenarios (rush hour, incidents)
    \item \textbf{Throughput Improvement} $\geq 5\%$ --- total vehicles served across the 16-intersection network
    \item \textbf{Emissions Reduction} $\geq 10\%$ --- CO$_2$ proxy proportional to idling queue length
    \item \textbf{Safety Score} $\geq 60/100$ --- composite of near-miss rate and hard-stop frequency
    \item \textbf{MCDM Ranking Stability} --- WSM and TOPSIS produce consistent winner under $\pm$15\% weight perturbation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scope and Scenario Coverage}

Five traffic scenarios are implemented: \textbf{Normal} ($1.0\times$), \textbf{Rush Hour} ($2.5\times$ traffic multiplier), \textbf{Incident} (lane closure at DIFC with $1.4\times$ rerouting), \textbf{Event} ($2.0\times$ local boost near Dubai Mall/Marina), and \textbf{Bus Priority} ($1.2\times$ with 6 bus route preemption). Each runs for 300 training episodes $\times$ 200 steps = \textbf{15 experiments} total (3 agents $\times$ 5 scenarios). The 4$\times$4 grid uses real Dubai intersection names (Al Rigga, DIFC, Dubai Mall, Marina, etc.) grouped into four zones: Deira, Downtown, Jumeirah, and South Dubai.

% ======== 2. ALGORITHM ========
\section{Algorithm}

\subsection{End-to-End Simulation Pipeline}

Each training episode executes the following 6-stage pipeline via \texttt{simulation.py} [7]:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{clX}
    \toprule
    \textbf{\#} & \textbf{Module} & \textbf{Operation} \\
    \midrule
    1 & \texttt{environment.py} & Initialize 4$\times$4 grid; generate vehicle arrivals via Poisson process with 24-hr Dubai sinusoidal pattern \\
    2 & \texttt{agents.py} & Each agent observes 5D state $(q_{NS}, q_{EW}, \phi, t_g, p_{\text{nbr}})$ and selects action via $\varepsilon$-greedy policy \\
    3 & \texttt{environment.py} & Execute actions (HOLD/SWITCH/EXTEND); process traffic flow, queues, emissions, safety events \\
    4 & \texttt{environment.py} & Compute per-step reward using queue deltas, throughput, emissions, and balance penalty \\
    5 & \texttt{agents.py} & Update Q-tables: Q-Learning uses $\max Q(s',a')$; SARSA uses actual $Q(s',a')$; decay $\varepsilon$ \\
    6 & \texttt{mcdm.py} & Post-training: construct decision matrix; apply WSM + TOPSIS + sensitivity analysis [4][5] \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{State Space and Action Space}

Each intersection's state is a \textbf{5-dimensional} discretized tuple:

\begin{equation}
    s_i = (q_{NS}, q_{EW}, \phi, t_g, p_{\text{nbr}}) \quad \Rightarrow \quad |S| = 5 \times 5 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 450 \text{ states per intersection}
\end{equation}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{llcl}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Variable} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Range} & \textbf{Discretization} \\
    \midrule
    $q_{NS}$, $q_{EW}$ & Queue lengths (N--S, E--W) & $\{0..4\}$ & Bins of 5 vehicles \\
    $\phi$ & Current signal phase & $\{0, 1\}$ & 0~= NS green, 1~= EW green \\
    $t_g$ & Green timer category & $\{0, 1, 2\}$ & Short ($<$8), Medium (8--15), Long ($>$15) \\
    $p_{\text{nbr}}$ & Neighbor pressure & $\{0, 1, 2\}$ & Avg adjacent queue: Low/Med/High \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Neighbor pressure} provides upstream congestion awareness:
$p_{\text{nbr}}(i) = \text{discretize}\big(\frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}_i|} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} (q_j^{NS} + q_j^{EW})\big)$
where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of 2--4 grid-adjacent intersections. This gives RL agents an information advantage over Fixed Timer.

\textbf{Action space}: $A = \{0: \text{HOLD}, 1: \text{SWITCH}, 2: \text{EXTEND}\}$. Constraints: minimum green 10 steps before SWITCH; 3-step yellow phase during transitions; switch penalty $-0.5$ if green $< 8$ steps.

\subsection{Reward Function}

The reward for intersection $i$ at step $t$ uses \textbf{per-step deltas} for stable learning signals [3]:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    R_i^t = \underbrace{0.3(Q^{t-1} - Q^t)}_{\text{queue reduction}} - \underbrace{0.05\, Q^t}_{\text{queue penalty}} + \underbrace{0.4\, v_t}_{\text{throughput}} - \underbrace{1.0\, e_t}_{\text{emissions}} - \underbrace{0.08\, |q_{NS} - q_{EW}|}_{\text{balance}} + P_{\text{switch}}
}
\end{equation}

where $Q^t = q_{NS}^t + q_{EW}^t$ is total queue, $v_t$ = vehicles served this step, $e_t = 0.002 \times Q^t$ (CO$_2$ proxy), and $P_{\text{switch}} = -0.5$ if green duration $< 8$ steps. Using per-step queue deltas (not cumulative totals) ensures the reward remains informative across all 200 steps.

\subsection{RL Update Rules}

\textbf{Q-Learning} (off-policy): $Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \big[ r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s,a) \big]$

\textbf{SARSA} (on-policy): $Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \big[ r + \gamma Q(s', a') - Q(s,a) \big]$

Hyperparameters: $\alpha = 0.1$, $\gamma = 0.95$, $\varepsilon_0 = 1.0 \to \varepsilon_{\min} = 0.01$ with decay $\varepsilon \leftarrow \max(\varepsilon_{\min}, \varepsilon \times 0.995)$ per episode. Q-tables initialized to zeros.

\subsection{Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM)}

Post-training, agents are evaluated as alternatives across four criteria: Efficiency (throughput $\uparrow$, $w=0.35$), Safety (score $\uparrow$, $w=0.25$), Emissions (CO$_2$ $\downarrow$, $w=0.25$), Public Transport (bus delay $\downarrow$, $w=0.15$).

\textbf{WSM}: $S_i = \sum_j w_j \cdot \bar{r}_{ij}$ where $\bar{r}_{ij}$ is min--max normalized (inverted for cost criteria) [4].

\textbf{TOPSIS} [5]: (1) Vector normalize: $\bar{r}_{ij} = r_{ij} / \sqrt{\sum_k r_{kj}^2}$. (2) Weight: $v_{ij} = w_j \bar{r}_{ij}$. (3) Ideal $A^+$ and anti-ideal $A^-$. (4) Euclidean distances $D_i^+, D_i^-$. (5) Closeness: $C_i = D_i^- / (D_i^+ + D_i^-)$. Higher $C_i$ = better alternative.

\textbf{Sensitivity analysis}: Each criterion weight varied $\pm 0.15$ in 5 steps; remaining delta redistributed uniformly. Rankings stable under $\pm 10\%$ perturbation are deemed robust.

% ======== 4. DESCRIPTION OF DATA ========
\section{Description of Data}

\subsection{Data Classification}

This system uses \textbf{synthetically generated data} calibrated to Dubai traffic statistics. No external real-world dataset is ingested. All operational data is deterministically produced by \texttt{generate\_synthetic.py} with seed 42, ensuring bit-for-bit reproducibility. Five CSV datasets are generated:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lcX}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{Records} & \textbf{Key Columns} \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{intersection\_traffic.csv} & $\sim$345K & date, hour, intersection\_id, vehicle\_count, avg\_speed, congestion\_level, queue\_ns/ew \\
    \texttt{vehicle\_detections.csv} & $\sim$580K & timestamp, intersection\_id, vehicle\_type, speed, lane, direction \\
    \texttt{emergency\_events.csv} & $\sim$500 & timestamp, intersection\_id, event\_type, response\_time, preemption\_time \\
    \texttt{congestion\_index.csv} & $\sim$259K & date, hour, zone, congestion\_index, avg\_speed, co2\_emissions \\
    \texttt{signal\_timing.csv} & 144 & intersection\_id, time\_period, ns/ew\_green\_sec, cycle\_length \\
    \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\textbf{Calibration}: Vehicle distribution: 40\% sedan, 25\% SUV, 12\% taxi, 10\% truck, 8\% bus, 3\% motorcycle, 2\% emergency. Location-specific factors: Downtown $1.4\times$, DIP $0.7\times$, Airport $1.3\times$. Weekend $0.7\times$ reduction. 24-hr sinusoidal pattern with AM/PM peaks [6].

\subsection{Key Data Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Synthetic only}: No EM-DAT, RTA, or real sensor data --- performance claims are non-generalisable to production
    \item \textbf{Deterministic}: Seed 42 produces identical runs; no stochastic robustness analysis
    \item \textbf{No spatial model}: Queues are scalar proxies, not computed from road geometry or GIS routing
    \item \textbf{Simplified emissions}: CO$_2 = 0.002 \times$ queue length; real emissions depend on vehicle type, idle RPM, temperature
\end{itemize}

% ======== 5. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS ========
\section{Experimental Results}

\subsection{Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{RL excels in dynamic scenarios}: SARSA reduces wait time by \textbf{14.6\%} and emissions by \textbf{14.6\%} in Rush Hour vs.\ Fixed Timer. Q-Learning achieves \textbf{+5.0\%} throughput improvement.
    \item \textbf{Fixed Timer adequate for predictable traffic}: In Normal conditions, all three agents perform similarly (wait time within 8\%). This is expected and honest --- predictable traffic needs no adaptation.
    \item \textbf{SARSA more conservative}: Consistently lower wait times than Q-Learning across all scenarios, with marginally higher safety scores, consistent with on-policy learning [3].
    \item \textbf{Incident response}: SARSA achieves \textbf{21.8\%} wait reduction and \textbf{21.8\%} emission reduction during lane-closure incidents --- the strongest RL advantage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{User Interface --- Data Visualisation Dashboard}

\subsection{Dashboard Overview}

The dashboard is implemented as a \textbf{Vite + React 18} single-page application with a dark operations-centre theme (background \texttt{\#09090b}, multi-color section accents). The UI loads 7 pre-computed JSON files from \texttt{public/data/} --- no runtime backend is required. Each of the 5 tabs presents a distinct analytical view. Deployed on Vercel at: \url{https://smart-traffic-management.vercel.app}. Source: \url{https://github.com/KartikJoshi23/smart-traffic-management}.

    \caption{Dashboard Landing Page --- Overview Tab with KPI Cards, Economic Impact Analysis, and 4$\times$4 Dubai Intersection Grid}
    \label{fig:dashboard}

\subsection{Tab-by-Tab Description}

\textbf{Tab 1 --- Dashboard (Overview):} Four KPI cards (throughput, wait reduction, emissions, safety) with section-colored accent borders. Economic Impact panel translates simulation metrics to AED savings using Dubai-specific parameters (Value of Time AED~2.08/min, fuel idle 0.8~L/hr, CO$_2$ 2.31~kg/L) [6]. SVG mini-map of the 4$\times$4 intersection grid with labeled Dubai zone names.

\textbf{Tab 2 --- Live Simulation:} Full SVG road network with 16 clickable intersection nodes. Congestion-colored borders (green/amber/red). Animated vehicle dots flowing between intersections. NS/EW queue bars with labels. Agent toggle (Q-Learning / SARSA / Fixed Timer) uses \textit{trained} Q-tables. \textbf{XAI Decision Panel}: shows Q-values for all 3 actions, confidence \% (Q-value differential), and human-readable reasoning. \textbf{Chaos Mode}: Sandstorm ($-$45\% capacity), Sensor Failure (corrupted readings), Emergency Vehicle (signal preemption). Play/Pause/Step controls with 1--5$\times$ speed.

\textbf{Tab 3 --- Training Analysis:} Area charts with gradient fills for reward, wait time, throughput, and epsilon decay across 300 episodes. Per-scenario toggle. Summary statistics from last 50 episodes.

\textbf{Tab 4 --- Scenario Comparison:} Color-coded heatmap: 5 scenarios $\times$ 3 agents $\times$ 4 metrics. Improvement percentages vs.\ Fixed Timer with directional indicators. Best-agent highlighting.

\textbf{Tab 5 --- MCDM Evaluation:} Criteria weight bars, WSM/TOPSIS ranking tables with score bars, radar chart (all alternatives overlaid), WSM vs TOPSIS grouped bar chart, sensitivity analysis table with stability badges. \textbf{Export}: Download results as CSV or JSON.
